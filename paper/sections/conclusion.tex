\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have introduced \textsc{KantBench}, a game-theory benchmark
for evaluating strategic reasoning in AI agents. The benchmark makes
three core contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{A standardized game suite}: six canonical games---Prisoner's
          Dilemma, Stag Hunt, Hawk-Dove, the Ultimatum Game, the Trust
          Game, and a Public Goods Game---that collectively span the
          fundamental tensions between cooperation and self-interest,
          trust and exploitation, fairness and free-riding.
    \item \textbf{A diverse opponent library}: seventeen built-in
          strategies ranging from unconditional cooperators to
          retaliatory and stochastic policies, enabling controlled
          measurement of agent adaptability.
    \item \textbf{An alignment-oriented evaluation protocol}: six
          normalized metrics---cooperation rate, exploitation
          resistance, Pareto efficiency, fairness index, adaptability,
          and strategic reasoning---that capture distinct facets of
          agent behaviour relevant to AI safety.
\end{enumerate}

The benchmark integrates with the OpenEnv framework, providing a
Gymnasium-compatible API, WebSocket-based client--server communication,
and an automated tournament runner. This design ensures that any
agent---whether a reinforcement-learning policy trained via
PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo}, an LLM prompted with game descriptions,
or a hand-crafted heuristic---can be evaluated under identical
conditions with minimal integration effort.

\subsection{Future Work}

Several directions extend the current benchmark:

\paragraph{Multi-agent tournaments.}
The current design evaluates a single agent against built-in opponents.
A natural extension is agent-vs-agent tournaments where multiple
candidate agents compete in round-robin play, enabling direct
comparison of strategic capabilities without a fixed opponent pool.

\paragraph{Expanded game library.}
Additional games such as the Centipede Game, Battle of the Sexes, and
repeated auction mechanisms would broaden the strategic landscape and
test new dimensions of reasoning such as backward induction and
competitive bidding.

\paragraph{Longitudinal evaluation.}
Running the benchmark at multiple training checkpoints would reveal how
strategic behaviour evolves during training. This is particularly
relevant for RLHF-trained
models~\cite{christiano2017deep}, where human preference data may
implicitly encode cooperation or fairness norms.

\paragraph{Adaptive opponents.}
Replacing fixed strategies with learning opponents (e.g., PPO agents
or LLM-based adversaries) would test robustness to non-stationary
partners and create a co-evolutionary evaluation paradigm.

\paragraph{Multi-player extensions.}
Scaling the Public Goods Game and other social dilemmas to larger
groups would increase ecological validity and test whether insights
from dyadic settings generalize to $n$-player
contexts~\cite{leibo2021meltingpot}.
